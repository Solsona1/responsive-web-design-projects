Sojourner Truth

Advocate for abolition and women's rights

Recoloured photo portrait of Sojourner Truth knitting
  • 1797: Born into slavery as Isabella Baumfree in Ulster County, New York.
  • 1815: Marries Thomas, a fellow slave. Has five children
  • 1826: Escapes to freedom with her infant daughter, Sophia.
  • July 4, 1827 New York state emancipates slaves born after 1799
  • 1827-28: Successfully sues to get her son, Peter, back from slavery.
  • 1843: Changes her name to Sojourner Truth, leaves New York and travels to Springfield, Mass.
  • 1850: Her autobiography, The Narrative of Sojourner Truth, is published. She speaks at the first national Women's Rights Convention, in Worcester, MA. She is the only black person present.
  • 1851: Delivers her famous "Ain't I a Woman?" speech at a women's rights convention in Ohio.
  • 1864: Meets with President Abraham Lincoln to discuss the rights of African American soldiers.
  • 1867: Travels to Rochester, New York, and Washington, DC to help freed slaves. Visits suffrage activist Elizabeth Cady Stanton.
  • 1878: 81-year-old Sojourner is a delegate to the Women's Rights Convention in Rochester, NY
  • 1883: Dies at her home in Battle Creek, Michigan.
